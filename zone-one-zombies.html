Zone One Zombies

Zone One by Colson Whitehead


Summary of Zone One

Colson Whitehead's Zone One occurs in a post-apocalyptic world that centers on a zombie hunter, Mark Spitz. The postmodern world still has characteristics like consumerism and multimedia, now in conjunction with a zombie outbreak. The story takes place in Manhattan and follows the survivors' efforts to rebuild humanity.

Characteristics

“They were a succession of imponderable tableaux, the malfunctioning stragglers and the places they chose to haunt throughout the Zone and beyond. An army of mannequins, limbs adjusted by an inscrutable hand. The former shrink, plague-blind, sat in her requisite lounge chair, feet up on the ottoman, blank attentive face waiting for the patient who was late, ever late, and unpacking the reasons for this would consume a large portion of a session that would never occur” (Whitehead 46).

The zombies in Zone One are called skels. They are primarily characterized as pale and leucistic. Notable symptoms include loss of logic to a mindless state that constantly tries to attack survivors and likes to be in herds. The second kind of skels, known as the stragglers, are almost in this catatonic state, in which they are not as aggressive and usually repeat tasks that could be related to their previous life. In both instances, the skels slowly decay.

Connection to its Culture

The active skels are characterized by their horde-like nature, embodying the fear of mob mentality and the absence of individuality in a crowd. This serves as a commentary on culture in the United States. While the U.S. is celebrated as a cultural melting pot, this blending can often dilute the unique qualities of its constituent cultures, losing the essence of the distinctiveness that built it. Similarly, the skels symbolize a loss of individuality, evoking an underlying sadness. The narrator perceives them as people who once had hopes and dreams, now stripped of what made them unique, blending into an indistinguishable mass. Zone One uses the skels to critique postmodern fears of losing identity, being reduced to a mere cog in the machine, or being a drop in the ocean. They highlight the dangers of a reality where diversity becomes homogenized, erasing the richness of difference.
